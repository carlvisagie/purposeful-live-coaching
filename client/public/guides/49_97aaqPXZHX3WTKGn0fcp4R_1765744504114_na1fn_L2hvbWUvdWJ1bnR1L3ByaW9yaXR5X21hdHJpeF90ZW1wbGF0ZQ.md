# Priority Matrix Template: The Eisenhower Method for Peak Productivity

## Purpose and Introduction

The **Priority Matrix**, often referred to as the **Eisenhower Matrix** or **Urgent-Important Matrix**, is a powerful time management and decision-making tool designed to help you distinguish between tasks that are **urgent** and those that are **important** [1]. By classifying your tasks into four distinct quadrants, you can effectively prioritize your workload, reduce stress, and focus your energy on activities that drive long-term goals and meaningful results. This template is designed to be immediately usable, guiding you from task identification to focused action.

## The Four Quadrants Explained

The matrix is built on two axes: **Urgency** (tasks that demand immediate attention) and **Importance** (tasks that contribute to your long-term mission, values, and goals).

| Quadrant | Name | Description | Focus |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **I** | **Do** (Urgent & Important) | Crises, pressing problems, deadlines. These require immediate action. | **Necessity & Crisis** |
| **II** | **Decide** (Not Urgent & Important) | Planning, relationship building, new opportunities, prevention. This is the quadrant of quality and personal leadership. | **Effectiveness & Quality** |
| **III** | **Delegate** (Urgent & Not Important) | Interruptions, some emails, some meetings, other people's minor issues. These should be minimized or outsourced. | **Deception & Busyness** |
| **IV** | **Delete/Drop** (Not Urgent & Not Important) | Time wasters, pleasant activities that yield no value, some busywork. These should be eliminated. | **Waste & Avoidance** |

## Clear Instructions for Use

Follow these three simple steps to complete your Priority Matrix:

1.  **List Your Tasks:** Write down all your current tasks, projects, and commitments in the "Task Description" column of the table below.
2.  **Assess and Classify:** For each task, determine if it is **Urgent** (requires immediate attention) and **Important** (contributes to your long-term goals). Place a checkmark (or 'X') in the corresponding quadrant column (I, II, III, or IV).
3.  **Take Action:** Based on the quadrant, apply the recommended action (Do, Decide, Delegate, or Delete) and record your next concrete step in the "Action Plan" column.

## Evidence and References

The Priority Matrix is a core concept in time management, popularized by Stephen Covey in *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People* [2], based on a quote attributed to Dwight D. Eisenhower: "I have two kinds of problems, the urgent and the important. The urgent are not important, and the important are never urgent."
